Background

The core idea of this project was to replace the classical probabilistic divergence measure (forward/reverse KL most commonly) used in knowledge distillation pipelines and replace them with a measure of intermediate representational similarity, which can effectively be back propagated through. We ended up working with generalized (lienar) shape metrics as formulated in Williams, et. al. To compare with divergence measures, we stick to the last-layer of a BERT-like model. We showed performance improvements on a series of tasks on a Polish benchmark, when shape metrics and KL divergences are used together for knowledge distillation. Check out the report we made for a class project below for more details!
Knowledge Distillation Through RepresentationalSimilarity MetricsDima Zhylko Prajjwal BhattaraiAbstractKnowledge distillation is a common paradigm for transferring capabilities from alarger model to smaller models. Assuming white box access to the larger model,traditional knowledge distillation methods often draw a probabilistic measureover the activations and minimize a divergence measure between the larger andsmaller model. These methods are often limited to last-layer activations, anddo not leverage any measure of similarity between the activations from hiddenlayers. In this work, we propose a method that uses shape metrics, which areconstructed to account for invariances to certain isometry groups in the space ofneural network representations. We construct a novel way to compute shape metricfor a single layer representations that can be efficiently backpropagated, and applyit for distillation of a large language model. We show that using shape metrics ondistilling the last layer of a HERBERT model outperforms standard KL divergencebased distillation across various benchmarks on the KLEJ benchmarks. 11 Background1.1 Knowledge DistillationWhile large models are achieving state-of-the-art results across almost all vision and languagetasks, their inherently massive computational and cost requirements prohibits efficient access to the"emergent" abilities that are encapsulated in them. Knowledge Distillation (KD) is one of the manycurrent paradigms that aims to bridge the gap between size and performance by inducing ways oftransferring knowledge and abilities from a larger, complex model to a smaller, more efficient one.(Xu et al. [2024] )Knowledge Variants exist in the exact "knowledge" that is being distilled from the teacher to thestudent model. Assuming black-box to the model itself (which is common for larger proprietarymodels), we can use labels generated by the teacher (Hsieh et al. [2023], He et al. [2023]), essentiallyturning an unsupervised problem for the student into a supervised problem with labels generatedthrough the teacher model. A more sophisticated approach incorporates feedback generated bythe teacher model with respect to the student’s output. (Agarwal et al. [2024], Gu et al. [2024]).However, assuming white-box access to the teacher model, we can leverage alignment of the teacher-student model through not just their outputs, but also their intermediary representations and latentfeatures. Prior works utilize last-layer activations and minimize probabilistic divergences (for soft-max scores) (Hinton et al. [2015], Wen et al. [2023]) or Euclidean distances between student andteacher activations. (Sanh et al. [2020], Liang et al. [2023]). Our work aims to design a frameworkthat allows for intermediary features in any arbitrary hidden layer of a neural network to be alignedbetween teacher and student models. We focus on cases where the student model is minimized overthe masked language modelling loss, (Devlin et al.
