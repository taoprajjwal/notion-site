Classifying EEG using shape measures

Background

Instead of intermediary representations of neural networks, which is my main research focus, in this project, I experimented with representational measures for neural signals.The core idea here is to use the metric properties of shape metrics for classifying EEG singals using simple clustering and regression models. While this line of analysis has been done for individual neural signals, we wanted to explore how noisier and lower resolution signals like EEG would handle a similar analysis. EEG decoding using Representation Similarity AnalysisAnsal Arimbassery1 and Prajjwal Bhattarai11New York UniversityAbstractWe use the recently proposed (stochastic) shape metrics as the similarity measure for training simple machinelearning models on EEG signals. We use a dataset of EEG signals consisting of 26 subjects performing a discriminationtask between two stimuli and evaluate our model on its ability to decode the stimuli given a cross-subject EEGstimuli. We show that shape metrics outperform Euclidean distance as a measure of representational similarity,and stochastic shape metrics outperform linear shape on EEG decoding accuracy. We include the code for datasetconstruction and analysis. 11 IntroductionElectroencephalography (EEG) is a noninvasive brain imaging technique with a high temporal resolution.While the ease and portability of EEG make it a suitable medium for imaging, noise is a consistent issuewhile working with EEG signals. EEG decoding is a elementary problem in neuroinformatics, usuallyinvolving the design of a model to predict the stimuli/condition from an EEG signal. (Kriegeskorte andDouglas [2019])Representational Similarity Analysis is a framework that aims to establish a similarity metric forcomparisons of representations of neural signals across various modalities and stimuli. Establishthis framework, with a particular focus on preserving invariances in the representational space, isa common problem with obvious implications in neuroscience, but also a significant importance inprobing deep learning based neural networks.(Barrett et al. [2019], Klabunde et al. [2023]). Classicalrepresentational similarity metrics in neuroscience are usually measured by considering the second-orderisomorphism of representations on a first-order similarity structure: the Representational DissimilarityMatrices.(Kriegeskorte et al. [2008]) This results in a metric that, while usually invariant to translations,is not invariant to rotations or permutations, and does not follow the standard properties of a distancefunction such as the triangle inequality. This is in contrast with approaches in machine learning, that havetraditionally focused on measures based on alignment or correlation analysis (Raghu et al. [2017]), oftenresulting in a similarity measure that follows the property of a distance function, but without an explicitcontrol on the geometrical invariances. Following the properties of a distance function is important for asimilarity metric, since doing so opens up standard geomteric analysis tools like clustering and kernelmethods. Shape metrics (Williams et al. [2021], Duong et al. [2023]) are a recently proposed extensionof alignment based similarity measures, that enforce invariance in the measure with respect to certaintransformation group and follow the triangle inequality.Shape metrics have almost exclusively been used with data collected from implanted extracellularmicro-electrode arrays.
